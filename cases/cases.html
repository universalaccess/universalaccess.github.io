Large-Scale Master Planning Using Least-Cost System Designs


2018-2020 Rwanda National Electrification Stratgies and associated Plans (NESP 2024)

[pdf] The government of Rwanda recognizes the vital role that electricity access plays in accelerating economic development through improving health and standards of living. Energy and particularly access to electricity is Government’s key priority. This is why significant investments have been made and progress registered led to over 40.5% of households getting access to electricity by August 2017 and why the government has set the target of Universal Electrification for the year 2024.

The aforementioned initiative to extend access to electricity involves a coordinated effort across all power sector participants to connect new customers, focusing also on powering productive activities.

The first phase of this project defined the “Grid Standard Service Area”. According to the institutional design established by REG – EDCL in the project, this Grid Standard Service Area comprises (a) the customers selected for a connection to the Rwandan Central Distribution Network and (b) the customers that will receive their electricity supply through an isolated grid-compatible microgrid. Outside the Grid Standard Service Area, the least-cost achievement of Universal Access in Rwanda will also require the supply of DC Solar Kits for low-demand residential customers (below 50 Wp as defined by EDCL). It will also determine where a full-fledged stand-alone AC solar system should be provided to larger customers who are too isolated from the network and from other customers to techno-economically justify an individual connection to the central grid or an off-grid microgrid. Per village least-cost choices, designs, a full geospatial database of MV and LV layouts and a complete bill of materials for grid and off-grid systems have been delivered in this first phase.

The second phase included the development of an Implementation Plan for the MV and LV distribution infrastructure and the on- and off-grid projects that are required to connect additional customers so that universal electricity access can be achieved in 2024. This excludes villages where “densification” (small distance and fill-in connections to the aleady existing grid) is the required electrification mode. In addition to a sound estimation of the investment and operation costs, the results obtained make possible to inform prospective off-grid investors about what areas are not contemplated for grid extension for the temporal scope of the plan (now to 2024). The detailed results of our study will also inform the implementation of the National Electrification Strategy (NES) and of the National Electrification Plan (NEP). The electrification ratios resulting from the Master Plan in this project represent significantly increased efforts compared with the past. This implies a substantial development of additional financial and institutional resources in the future, in particular as rural electrification is extended into more remote areas. The last deliverable report in this phase reexamined the ESSP energy strategy in connection with the institutions responsible for the implementation of the electrification plan. It provides comments on the proposed implementation of the plan and the potential financing alternatives.

Proposal to achieve universal electricity access in Colombian rural isolated communities

Aunque Colombia tiene una cobertura elevada de electrificación, superior al promedio de América Latina y el Caribe – de cada 100 hogares colombianos, 97 cuentan con servicio de energía eléctrica – todavía quedan más de 400.000 viviendas sin servicio, de las cuales algo más de la mitad se encuentran en zonas colindantes al Sistema Interconectado Nacional (SIN) y el resto en Zonas No Interconectadas (ZNI), en su mayor parte en zonas de difícil acceso y donde es costoso proporcionar el servicio eléctrico.

La electrificación en estas zonas requiere modelos de negocio nuevos que puedan utilizar eficazmente las nuevas tecnologías. Este trabajo encargado por el Gobierno de Colombia al Profesor Ignacio Pérez Arriaga en Junio de 2019 y entregado en Marzo de 2020, constituye una oportunidad para desarrollar estos modelos y la regulación que los hará posibles. En otros aspectos del proyecto – calidad del servicio eléctrico y desarrollo de un proyecto piloto para la planificación de la electrificación – han colaborado los investigadores Pablo Dueñas y Andrés González, respectivamente. El aumento de cobertura se debe caracterizar por soluciones sostenibles en el tiempo, compatibles con un modelo de energización de largo plazo, y adaptadas a las condiciones de las zonas no interconectadas en Colombia.

Se trata de definir los marcos legales y los modelos de negocio que puedan atraer capital privado y público para electrificar zonas rurales no interconectadas y relativamente aisladas. Para ello se ha partido de las mejores experiencias internacionales, con algunos ingredientes nuevos y adaptadas al caso particular de Colombia. El enfoque propuesto está en línea con el “Integrated Distribution Framework”, propuesto por la Global Commission to End Energy Poverty en el Inception Report publicado en Septiembre de 2019.

Las recomendaciones para el aumento de cobertura en condiciones sostenibles se han estructurado en tres bloques: i) la necesidad de una planificación integrada de referencia; ii) un marco regulatorio que permita la escalabilidad y la movilización de todos los esfuerzos necesarios para la universalización del servicio y su sostenibilidad a largo plazo; y iii) un adecuado marco institucional y de gobernanza eléctrica.

La planificación de la electrificación de un territorio, por cualquier modo de suministro –extensión de la red, mini-redes o sistemas fotovoltaicos aislados – es necesaria para determinar la hoja de ruta de inversiones e implementación de proyectos para el logro de las metas de electrificación con el menor costo posible. También permite establecer las prioridades de inversión para cada uno de los distintos tramos y fases de implementación del plan, conforme a la disponibilidad de fondos y a las políticas prioritarias energéticas y de desarrollo sostenible, así como determinar los costos eficientes que pueden servir de base para el cálculo de las tarifas y subsidios necesarios para la regulación y sostenibilidad del suministro eléctrico a largo plazo.

Para conseguir una cobertura del servicio eléctrico completa y sostenible en las ZNI es necesario atraer inversión, pero especialmente privada. Esto requiere un modelo viable de negocio, con una remuneración regulada suficiente y seguridad jurídica, que necesariamente comprende una razonable estabilidad del régimen regulatorio en el tiempo. Este modelo de negocio adoptado debe ser compatible con una visión de futuro que garantice una estructura y funcionamiento adecuados del sector eléctrico a medio y largo plazo.

El modelo de negocio adoptado debe integrar los diversos modos de electrificación que resultan en el mínimo costo de suministro para el nivel de demanda existente en cada momento, de acuerdo con un proceso previo de planificación de la electrificación en el territorio considerado. Y debe garantizar que ningún potencial consumidor permanezca sin servicio. Los operadores de red deben estar autorizados a realizar inversiones en soluciones aisladas bajo las mismas condiciones remuneratorias que hoy tienen para la extensión por red.

Los anteriores requisitos conducen a proponer un modelo de concesión territorial con responsabilidad de servicio universal en la zona asignada, tanto como suministrador por defecto como de último recurso (en caso de que otro suministrador existente en la zona abandone), pero solamente en exclusividad para el suministro por extensión de la red interconectada, y no en exclusividad para soluciones aisladas, ya sea con micro-redes o con sistemas individuales.

Se busca así conseguir la universalidad del servicio, reducir el riesgo del inversor (y consiguientemente el costo de capital) así como garantizar la permanencia del suministro en el tiempo (lo que además permite distribuir las anualidades de pago en plazos mayores, reduciendo las tarifas para los consumidores finales). Esto elimina diversos problemas creados por otros enfoques regulatorios, aunque, por supuesto, sin eliminar la necesidad de un subsidio, que siempre ha sido necesario – de una u otra forma – en la electrificación rural. Y no es suficiente con proporcionar subsidios a la inversión inicial, sino que los subsidios – directos o cruzados – deben continuar en el tiempo para cubrir los costes de reposición, administración, operación y mantenimiento, cuando la recaudación vía tarifas a los clientes locales no sea suficiente.



Indonesia: Least-cost electrification plan for Papua, Papua Barat, Maluku and Maluku Utara.

The Government of Indonesia aims to achieve near-universal electricity access by 2020. Although progress towards this goal has been impressive, approximately 5.9 million households across the country remain without electricity. The provinces of Eastern Indonesia, and in particular Papua, have the lowest electrification ratios in the country and are therefore a focus of the Government’s electrification efforts. Indonesia's electrification push goes hand-in-hand with endeavors towards increasing in the share of renewable generation in the country. In many cases, renewable technologies comprise both the least cost and most sustainable options for the electrification of small islands and remote areas.

The Asian Development Bank (ADB) has provided prior technical assistance to the government on the least cost options for electrification of Maluku-Papua and now is continuing this work by supporting our research on mini and micro-grid development in the region, which will culminate in a formal plan for the region.

The South Service Territory in Uganda: Large scale case studies

The rural population in Uganda is approximately 84% of the country’s total population (Trading Economics, 2014). The national electrification rate is 15%, and only 7% of the rural population has access to electricity (IEA, World Energy Outlook 2015). As a result, a large focus of the electrification efforts in Uganda is on providing rural electrification solutions.

The Universal Access Team is interacting with Ugandan rural electrification agencies, utilities, private sector partners, and NGOs to develop a method for providing electricity access to unconnected rural consumers. REM has been used to identify areas of the country most appropriate for certain technologies (i.e. grid extensions, microgrids, or standalone systems). REM will continue to be used in this context for the design of additional infrastructure and reinforcements to accommodate more rapid electrification.


Vaishali, Bihar, India: Large scale case studies

We are engaged in producing a detailed case study of optimal electrification strategies for the district of Vaishali in Bihar, India. We have worked closely with the local regulators and distribution companies to develop a comprehensive view of the existing infrastructure to inform our use of REM. Vaishali is a mostly agricultural region where many customers remain without electricity access, though the grid has been expanding rapidly in recent years. Nevertheless, grid reliability remains poor, making microgrids attractive in some parts of the district; microgrids may be better able to consistently meet demand at peak times. Preliminary results have suggested that the optimal mix of electrification in Vaishali depends quite heavily on the future reliability of the central grid.

In order to help us to tune REM and ensure our model parameters are accurate, we are working with Tata Power DDL to build two microgrid pilots in Vaishali. We anticipate construction to start in the coming months and we look forward to seeing the positive impact of this work!


Kenya and Colombia: Rural Electrification planning with REM. ENEL Foundation

The object of the project is to use the Reference Electrification Model (REM) to establish the potential for microgrid business models in a region of Colombia and in a region of Kenya, subject to the requirements and specifications of ENEL Greenpower. REM will be able to perform objective quantitative assessments about the most convenient way to provide electricity service to each household, comparing together the three major electrification modes: 1) extend the existing grid; 2) build isolated grids with local generation (micro grids); and 3) install isolated stand-alone systems on each target building.

1. Colombia: A test case will be carried out in a small zone of a peri-urban study region, with around 3,000 unelectrified households, and evaluated by Enel SpA in order to understand the optimal electrification solutions.

2. Kenya: A test case will be carried out in a small zone of a study region in rural area, with no grid available and around 15,200 households, and evaluated by Enel Green Power in order to understand the optimal electrification solutions

Mini-grids with Thermoacoustic Engine

The Thermo Acoustic Power System (TAPS) technology addresses the remote electrification challenge by providing clean, reliable, and maintenance-free power. The TAPS architecture is based on a thermoacoustic engine that improves the Stirling engine with enhanced flexibility and longevity. The architecture is modular in design and benefits from combining several engines to produce a scalable system. The objective of this study is evaluating technically and economically the TAPS technology in comparison to the current generation technologies, i.e. solar plus battery and diesel, in particular when the optimal electrification mode is either the microgrid or the standalone system.

Nigeria: Microgrid Designs with the World Bank, Odyssey Energy Solutions, REA, and General Electric

With research funding from General Electric, our team has been working with the World Bank, the Rural Electrification Agency (REA) – Nigeria, and Odyssey Energy Solutions to design microgrids for Nigeria using LREM. The work focuses on 100 sites within five states including Sokoto, Ogun, Cross River, Plateau, and Niger. We have used LREM to design both generation and network assets for each microgrid based on customized demand/equipment/cost inputs from REA. We are using LREM to produce a bill of materials and are collaborating with the Odyssey platform to provide detailed financial analyses for each microgrid and assess the economic viabilities of individual projects.
